Streamline the Idea-To-Output pipeline.

Too many students want to build a project yet struggle to turn ideas into reality. Here is a guided pathway from blank-page paralysis to a real, finishable project. Pick a field, narrow a focus, set a scope, and walk away with a brief. Plus, foundational papers if you want them.

Step 01

Which field draws you in?

Pick the discipline that genuinely makes you curious. The best projects come from real interest.

Step 01 / Custom path

Name your field.

A "field" is a recognized area of study or practice with its own vocabulary, journals, and conferences. If you can't name yours yet, work through these prompts.

What problems do you find yourself thinking about when no one's assigning them?
The questions that follow you around in the shower or on walks. These tend to point toward fields, not just topics.
Which classes, books, or YouTube channels held your attention longest?
If you can't stop watching it, you've probably found a field. Check the description — is it called economics, philosophy of mind, urban planning, music theory? That label is your field.
Whose work do you wish you could do?
Look up someone you admire. Their academic department, job title, or self-description is probably a field name. Steal it.
Sanity check: does this field have a Wikipedia page, a university department somewhere, or recurring conferences?
If yes, you have a real field. If no, what you have is a topic — narrow it differently or pick the parent discipline.

Step 02 / Custom path

Narrow it to a focus.

Inside your field, find one specific corner. A whole field is a career; a sub-area is a project.

Open the Wikipedia page for your field and scroll to "subdisciplines" or "branches."
This is the fastest way to see the standard sub-area names. Pick one that genuinely interests you, not one that sounds impressive.
Find a university course catalog in your field and skim the upper-division class titles.
Each class title is a sub-area. Stanford, MIT, and Berkeley have public catalogs. The class names are pre-vetted by people who teach this stuff.
Check the section names of a major conference or journal in your field.
Conferences are organized by sub-area for a reason — those are the live, active corners where people are actually building things.
Stress-test: can you imagine spending 3+ weeks on this sub-area without getting bored?
If not, narrow further, broaden slightly, or pick a different sub-area. Honest answers here save you weeks later.
Step 03

How far do you want to push it?

Be honest about your time and current skill. Easy projects finished beat ambitious ones abandoned.

    iii.

    Foundational papers

    Read in this order. The first paper sets the language and core problem; the next two push you toward how the field thinks today.

    Since you're on a build-your-own path, I can't hand you three pre-vetted papers. But finding your own is a skill worth learning — and the steps are the same regardless of field.

    1. Start with a survey

    Search Google Scholar or arXiv for survey of your sub-area or review of your sub-area. A good survey paper saves weeks: it names the canonical works, summarizes the open problems, and gives you a citation map of the entire sub-field. Read one survey before reading anything else.

    2. Find foundational papers via citation tracking

    Once you have a survey, open connectedpapers.com or semanticscholar.org and paste in its title. You'll see a visual citation graph. The most-cited papers in your your sub-area graph are usually the foundational ones — that's what "foundational" means in practice: everyone cites them.

    3. Search arXiv strategically

    Go to arxiv.org and find the category matching your work. If your field isn't on arXiv, try Google Scholar, JSTOR (humanities, social science), PubMed (life sciences), or SSRN (economics, finance, law). Search with "your sub-area" plus a specific technical term. Sort by date for recent work; sort by citations for foundational work.

    4. Pick three and stop

    Pick three: one that defines the problem (often an older, highly-cited paper), one that introduces the dominant approach today, and one recent paper that represents the frontier. More than three is procrastination dressed up as research. Save the rest in a folder and read them if and when you need to.

    5. If you can't find papers, it's a signal

    If serious searching turns up nothing, two possibilities: either your sub-area has a different name than you think (try the prior-art audit again with synonyms), or you've stumbled into something genuinely under-explored. The first is much more common. The second is exciting — but verify the first before assuming the second.

    iv.

    Using papers without drowning

    1. Three-pass reading
      First pass: read abstract, intro, conclusion, and figure captions only — under 20 minutes. Second pass: read fully, look at the proofs and math but don't get bogged down. Third pass: only if it's directly relevant, reproduce a result or work the math yourself.
    2. Extract one idea per paper
      Remember that research papers are written by people who dedicate large amount of time and effort into understanding their field (at least hopefully). Don't try to absorb everything and don't feel bad if you're overwhelmed. Write down the single contribution that's most useful for your project — a method, a metric, a problem framing, a baseline you can compare against.
    3. Cite, don't copy
      When you borrow a method or framing, name the paper in your write-up. This signals you're building on real work, not reinventing wheels poorly.
    4. Use papers as scope-cutters
      If a paper solved the hard part of your problem twenty years ago, use their result and focus your effort on what's still open. Papers are essentially a license to skip work.
